# Indigenous Data Ethics Reflection — FPIC + Indigenous Data Sovereignty (Goal 1)

## Scenario (based on the subject case study)
DesignR plans to generate high-volume AI “Indigenous Australian imagery” for stock libraries. They rely on publicly visible datasets for AI generation, do not collaborate directly with Indigenous artists/communities, and attempt mitigation through disclaimers and donations. The images are sold under a license allowing purchasers to edit them.

## Stakeholders and power dynamics (critical reflection)
Key stakeholders include Indigenous artists and communities, DesignR staff and management, stock platform buyers, and the broader public who may interpret these images as culturally legitimate. The ethical risk is amplified by power imbalance: the organisation controls the tools, distribution, and profits, while Indigenous communities carry the risk of cultural harm, misrepresentation, and loss of control over cultural expression.

A major ethical issue is **cultural authority**: even if content is AI-generated, it can still reproduce or imitate Indigenous cultural expressions. Without Indigenous leadership, the organisation may unintentionally use restricted motifs or present culturally unsafe representations. Disclaimers do not remove the harm if the outputs still commodify culture and identity.

## Indigenous Data Sovereignty and consent
Indigenous Data Sovereignty emphasises Indigenous rights to govern how data about Indigenous peoples and cultures is collected, used, and shared. “Publicly visible” does not equal consent. If the AI system is trained on social media images or Indigenous artists’ work without permission, it risks extracting value from Indigenous cultural labour without consent or benefit-sharing.

**Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC)** is central here. FPIC requires:
- **Free:** no pressure or coercion
- **Prior:** consent obtained before creation/distribution
- **Informed:** full transparency about how the images will be generated, sold, and reused
- **Consent:** the right to say yes/no and set conditions

In this scenario, FPIC is absent because there is no meaningful consultation or consent mechanism. Donations are not equivalent to consent because they do not provide control or authority over representation and use.

## Potential harms and impacts
- Cultural harm and misrepresentation (including restricted/sacred content being imitated)
- Economic harm (undercutting Indigenous artists and authentic cultural products)
- Loss of trust and reputational damage (for both the organisation and platforms)
- Downstream misuse enabled by editable licensing (harmful modifications and context collapse)

## What I would do in a real project (future-aligned approach)
If Indigenous culture, data, or communities are involved (directly or indirectly), I would apply an “ethics gate” early:
1) Identify whether Indigenous stakeholders are affected and whether Indigenous cultural expression/data is involved.
2) Engage the appropriate Indigenous-led stakeholders and seek FPIC before production.
3) Document provenance of data/training sources and confirm licensing/permissions.
4) Ensure Indigenous governance and cultural authority shape what is acceptable and what is not.
5) Put controls in place for distribution/licensing and complaints/takedown pathways.

This reflection shows that ethical practice is not only compliance; it is culturally safe stakeholder engagement that respects authority, consent, and governance—especially where Indigenous Australians may be affected by IT outcomes.
